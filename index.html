The Manual Transmission

The Car Enthusiast Comeback Story

Stick shift cars had been dying for decades. Automakers were slowly removing them from their lineups. Most consumers would opt for the automatic because it was more fuel efficient, easier to drive (especially in traffic), and more reliable. They were about to become extinct until 2021, when the pandemic hit and nostalgia for the past was born.

The Great Decline

From the 1980s onward, automatic transmissions became increasingly sophisticated and fuel-efficient. Consumer preferences shifted toward convenience, and manual transmissions began their long march toward obsolescence.

1980: 35% market share
2000: 12.4% market share
2021: 0.9% - All-time low

Fewer Options and EVs

Many automakers were going electric. The market was shrinking as cars got bigger, heavier, and more expensive. People preferred one car solutions, like SUVs and no one had the buying power to support a fun 2 door coupe weekend car.

Unfavorable Economics

Low demand made manual options expensive to develop, and manuals were too niche to make a profit. Manuals used to be faster than their automatic counterparts, but research and development had quickly improved the automatics from a performance perspective.

Convenience Culture

Consumers prioritized ease of use over engagement, and customers began to priortize convenience and ease-of-use, viewing cars more as point-A-to-B appliances than engaging machines.

The Unexpected Comeback

Post-pandemic life (2022 and onward) sparked a renewed appreciation and nostalgia for the manual transmission. In a world of Teslas taking over and cars losing their soul, the post pandemic brought about a loud craving for something real. A rebellion began, not with signs, but with shift knobs and 3 pedals. The #SaveTheManuals movement became a rally cry for those who refused to let the soul of driving die. It was a conscious choice to master a dying art, to feel the raw, mechanical connection that a computer could never replicate.

The Fight for Three Pedals

Like a mechanical watch or a vinyl record, the manual is a cherished analog experience. But choosing one today—knowing it's less efficient, objectively slower, and a hassle in traffic—isn't just a preference. It's a bold statement that the experience of driving will always matter more than the numbers.

What's Next?

The manual's comeback is a powerful statement, but it happens in the shadow of an all-electric future. What does it mean to be a driver, or a car enthusiast when internal combustion engines fade away?

The last Golden Years

Right now, car manufacturers are coming out with their final edition/ special edition models. The MK5 Supra, the Audi R8, the Porsche 911... these are all examples of the final fully internal combustion engines with a manual transmission before automakers go electric.

The Enthusiast's Legacy

The future is unknown. As enthusiasts continue to support manual transmissions, it becomes a premium for those who truly appreciate the experience. Automakers will be aware that the feeling of driving will always matter, even as we move into a world of electic SUVs.

The manual transmission's story is no longer about how we drive, but why.
